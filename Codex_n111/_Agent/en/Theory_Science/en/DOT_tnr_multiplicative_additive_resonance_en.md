## 1. Main Intuition

In TNR, additive and multiplicative readings should not be understood
as two independent branches of arithmetic.

More precisely:

- the **additive reading** expresses
  continuity of passage,
  witness-order,
  sequence,
  and closure through adjacency;
- the **multiplicative reading** expresses
  structural weaving,
  scaffold,
  carrier-field,
  and architectural articulation.

Then one and the same numerical node may have:

- an additive image;
- a multiplicative image;
- and an invariant that is not identical to either one taken separately.

In short:

> the additive side shows **how the structure proceeds**;  
> the multiplicative side shows **how the structure is assembled**.

### 1.1. Continuity, discreteness, and one structural material

This line should not be reduced to the banal opposition
of “addition versus multiplication.”

More precisely:

- the **additive** side in this corridor expresses **continuity of passage**;
- the **multiplicative** side expresses **continuity of structure**;
- **prime numbers** express **discrete invariants** that survive both readings.

So what appears here are not two, but three linked axes:

1. **passage**
   - witness-order,
   - adjacency,
   - local step,
   - additive continuity;
2. **structure**
   - scaffold,
   - splicing,
   - architectural articulation,
   - multiplicative continuity;
3. **invariant material**
   - primes,
   - irreducible bricks,
   - cancellation participants,
   - residues.

In short:

> the additive side answers how structure passes;  
> the multiplicative side answers how structure assembles;  
> the primes answer what remains irreducible across both readings.

### 1.2. Why the pair continuity / discreteness matters

If one looks only at the additive row,
structure appears to be a mere sequence of steps.

If one looks only at products,
structure appears to be a set of factorization patterns.

But in this corridor something else appears:

- continuity does not destroy discreteness;
- discreteness does not break continuity;
- prime numbers are not opposed to the flow,
  but pass through both flows as their shared material.

Therefore this document studies not arithmetic in the ordinary sense,
but **the resonance of continuity and discreteness on one prime-field**.

### 1.3. A visual split of the two flows

It is useful to keep the two flows visible from the start:

```text
additive flow:        1 -> 2 -> 3 -> 4 -> 5 -> ... -> p
multiplicative flow:  2 · 3 · 5 · 7 · ... · p
```

The first flow:

- is sequential;
- is held by adjacency;
- reads time, witness-order, and local step.

The second flow:

- does not progress by local neighboring steps;
- it constructs a scaffold through prime-selection;
- it reads structure, weaving, and carrier-articulation.

The central question is not which one is “right,”
but:

> where do they meet on the same material,
> and what survives after their comparison?

### 1.4. The natural row as a mechanism of invariant overlay

The natural row in this corridor should not be read
as merely an infinite linear sequence.

It should be read as a **mechanism of the sequential superposition of invariants**.

That is:

- every new structural invariant arises on some interval of the row;
- for a while it governs that interval as a stable law;
- later, inside a larger assembly,
  the same invariant is read as a partial case, a residue, or a component.

In short:

> each invariant is, for a while, the invariant of an interval;  
> later it becomes a variant against the background of a stronger invariant.

This does not mean that the invariant stops being invariant.
It means that it changes the **scale of its role** inside a larger machine.

Hence the natural row becomes not only a line of counting,
but a line of the sequential manifestation of structural laws.

## 4. The General Prime-Horizon Formula

### 4.0. Notation and visual split of the flows

Here the two flows should be separated explicitly:

- `n` — the natural index in the additive flow;
- `p` — the **prime-horizon**, the prime where both flows are compared;
- `q` — the running prime factor inside the multiplicative scaffold.

Then:

\[
A(p)=\sum_{n=1}^{p} n
\]

\[
M(p)=\prod_{\substack{q \le p \\ q \in \mathbb{P}}} q = p\#
\]

where:

- `A(p)` is the additive hull up to the prime-horizon `p`;
- `M(p)` is the multiplicative scaffold up to the same `p`.

Visually:

```text
natural flow:  1 -> 2 -> 3 -> ... -> p
prime flow:    2 · 3 · 5 · ... · p
                                ^
                                |
                        common prime-horizon
```

For an odd prime horizon `p`:

\[
A(p)=\frac{p(p+1)}{2}
\]

and if

\[
h=\frac{p+1}{2},
\]

then:

\[
A(p)=p\cdot h
\]

and therefore:

\[
\frac{M(p)}{A(p)}
=
\frac{p\#}{p\cdot h}
=
\frac{(p-1)\#}{h}.
\]

This is the main law of the corridor:

> additive hull acts as a cancellation mask on the multiplicative scaffold.

### 4.1. What exactly the half-step companion does

The number

\[
h=\frac{p+1}{2}
\]

should not be read as a merely technical divisor.

In this corridor it behaves as:

- a half-step companion;
- an additive witness-companion;
- a cancellation mask;
- a local measure of how the additive hull overlays the lower prime-field.

### 4.1.A. Scaffold-package cardinality as a third invariant

There is another node here that is easy to miss
if one looks only at magnitudes:

- `A(p)` as additive magnitude;
- `M(p)` as multiplicative magnitude.

But the multiplicative scaffold has another characteristic:

> how many connected support-nodes
> actually participate in the package up to a given horizon?

#### I. Pure prime-cardinality

If one counts only primes,
one gets:

\[
\pi(p)
\]

#### II. Anchor-extended support-cardinality

But in this corridor,
the scaffold is often read not as

\[

\]

but as the support-package

\[
1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdot \dots \cdot p.
\]

Then it is natural to introduce:

\[
\sigma(p)=1+\pi(p)
\]

and to rewrite it in a stronger form:

\[
N(p):=\sigma(p)=1+\pi(p).
\]

This `N(p)` may be read as:

- scaffold-dimension;
- support-dimension;
- the topological dimension of the scaffold in the current TNR-reading.

This is not yet a theorem about literal Euclidean dimension.
It means:

> `N(p)` expresses the cardinality of the connected support-basis,
> that is, how many independent support-nodes
> actually participate in a multiplicative assembly.
