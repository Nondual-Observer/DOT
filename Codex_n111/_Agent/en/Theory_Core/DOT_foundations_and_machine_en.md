## 1. Negative Foundation: Three Invariant Prohibitions

Let there exist a minimal **position of distinction**.  
This is not yet a subject, an object, a point in space, or a state.  
It is simply the minimal context in which it is permissible to say: "distinction takes place."

If such a position is stated at all, it can collapse in only three fundamental ways.  
Each mode of destruction is fixed as an absolute prohibition.

### 1.1. $FC^D$ — Prohibition of Coincidence

Distinction must not collapse into total identity.

If the distinguisher and the distinguished coincide, the distance between them becomes zero, and the distinction vanishes as a fact.  
This means coincidence is prohibited not as a physical event, but as a **logical collapse of distance**.

$FC^D$ maintains the very fact of the cut.

### 1.2. $FC^F$ — Prohibition of Non-Fixability

Distinction has no right to occur in such a way that no trace remains after it.

Even if distance was maintained, the distinction could become ghostly: the position after the act is indistinguishable from the position before it.  
In that case, the distinction is not fixed and logically does not exist.

$FC^F$ maintains the very fact of the trace.

### 1.3. $FC^C$ — Prohibition of External Grounding

Distinction has no right to justify itself by referring to an external meta-context that is not part of the distinction itself.

If the permissibility of a distinction is explained through an external observer level, an infinite regress of support arises: who legalizes this external level, and so on.

$FC^C$ maintains the very fact of the autonomy of distinction.

### 1.4. Why exactly three prohibitions?

The importance here lies not in historical choice, but in the completeness of the types of collapse.

If distinction is stated, it can be destroyed:

1. through the disappearance of distance;
2. through the disappearance of the trace;
3. through the disappearance of its own support.

These are three fundamentally different failures:

- coincidence;
- tracelessness;
- external dependence.

They are not derived from each other and are not variants of the same thing.

### 1.5. Borromean Connectivity of Prohibitions

These three prohibitions do not merely stand side-by-side. They form a minimal Borromean link.

- Remove $FC^D$ — distinction collapses into zero.
- Remove $FC^F$ — distinction dissolves without a trace.
- Remove $FC^C$ — distinction loses its ground and descends into an infinite external support.

None of the three replaces the other two.  
Only their joint maintenance makes distinction logically survivable.

At this level, there is not yet:

- space,
- time,
- object,
- axes,
- octahedron.

There is only the minimal stable fabric in which distinction **does not collapse**.

### 1.6. Formalization

For each position of distinction $x$, three Boolean predicates are introduced:

- $D(x) = 1$ means: distance is lost (prohibition of coincidence is collapsed);
- $F(x) = 1$ means: trace is lost (prohibition of non-fixability is collapsed);
- $C(x) = 1$ means: autonomy is lost (prohibition of external grounding is collapsed).

Then the admissibility predicate reads as follows:

$Adm(x) \iff \neg D(x) \land \neg F(x) \land \neg C(x)$

A position is admissible if and only if none of the three prohibitions is collapsed.  
Weakening any conjunct destroys admissibility:

$\forall i \in \{D, F, C\}: i(x) = 1 \implies \neg Adm(x)$

This is the formal Borromean connectivity.

### 1.7. Principle of Inheritance

At the subsequent levels, we will need an operator of level transition. For now, it is sufficient to fix only its requirement:

**the next level is obliged to inherit all the prohibitions of the previous one.**

$Adm_{n+1}(x) \iff Adm_n(x) \land \text{transition}_n(x) \text{ is defined}$

## 2. Legalization: How the First Positive Layer Arises from Prohibitions

Three absolute prohibitions hold distinction from collapse. But in themselves, they do not yet build anything.  
If all three prohibitions act only as rigid stop-signals, the machine remains "clamped": distinction does not break, but it also does not gain a local degree of freedom.

The next step is needed:

- prohibitions must not disappear, but be **redistributed by load**;
- one prohibition becomes the limit;
- the other two form a working corridor.

This is exactly how the layer of **permissions** emerges.

### 2.1. Two Statuses of Prohibition

At the level of legalization, each prohibition can be in one of two statuses.

**Active Status ($FC^a$)**

- the prohibition participates in the form-generation of the mode;
- it holds structural tension;
- it defines the shape of the permissible corridor.

**Limit Status ($FC^l$)**

- the prohibition does not participate in building the mode from within;
- it stands as a guardian of the limit;
- it prevents the mode from collapsing into an impermissible position.

Important:

- these are not "two different prohibitions";
- these are two modes of operation for the same prohibition;
- no prohibition can be turned off completely.

### 2.2. The Permission Formula

A permission reads as follows:

$R \equiv (FC^a \circ FC^a ; FC^l)$

Meaning of the formula:

- two prohibitions work as an active corridor;
- the third holds the limit;
- what arises is not the cancellation of a restriction, but a **legal degree of freedom** within it.

Since there are exactly three prohibitions, there are exactly three ways to choose the limit invariant.  
This means that instead of an arbitrary number of modes, exactly **three canonical permissions** arise.

### 2.3. Combinatorics: Why exactly 6 permissible modes?

Each prohibition has two possible statuses: active ($a$) and limit ($l$).  
Three prohibitions yield a space of $2^3 = 8$ formal configurations of load distribution.

The two extreme configurations are impermissible:

- $(a, a, a)$ — all three prohibitions are active, none stands at the limit $\to$ the working corridor is unbounded, distinction is not maintained;
- $(l, l, l)$ — all three prohibitions are in limit maintenance, none forms the fabric of the mode $\to$ the working corridor is absent.

Exactly **6 permissible configurations** remain, for example:

$(a, a, l)$, $(a, l, a)$, $(l, a, a)$ and their polar pairs.

They are grouped into three canonical permissions — by the choice of the limit prohibition.  
This result is not postulated but obtained by excluding the extremes from the status space.

### 2.4. The Three Permissions

#### $R^D$

If $FC^D$ becomes the limit, the machine primarily holds the boundary from collapsing.  
This is the mode of:

- maintenance,
- density,
- opacity,
- resistance to sticking (merging).

#### $R^F$

If $FC^F$ becomes the limit, the machine primarily holds clarity and the trace.  
This is the mode of:

- readability,
- manifestability,
- structural luminosity,
- appearance.

#### $R^C$

If $FC^C$ becomes the limit, the machine primarily holds internal autonomous mobility.  
This is the mode of:

- excitation,
- transfer,
- actualization,
- internal triggering.

### 2.5. Properties of the set $\{R^D, R^F, R^C\}$

The three permissions inherit Borromean connectivity from the prohibitions:

- no permission is derivable from another;
- none exists in isolation;
- removing the corresponding prohibition destroys the permission;
- permissions **do not have** orientation, sign, or direction — they are not yet axes.

### 2.6. Limits of Applicability for §2

At the level of legalization, the following are **not yet permitted**:

Permissions are not yet directions, but only **survivability configurations**.

## 3. Stabilization: The Birth of Axes, Poles, and Limits

**Stabilization** is a mode of prohibition action such that permission R:

- is **reproducible**;
- is **distinguishable** relative to other R;
- **does not collapse** upon repeated application.

Stabilization adds no new content. It fixes the form of the permission's existence.

> **Stabilization is a condition of repeatability, not change.**

The operation $\odot$ is introduced for the first time.

> **$\odot$ — the operation of limit maintenance.**

It indicates that an element is in the mode of **active presence as a boundary**, but does not participate in forming the fabric of the mode.

Comparison with previously introduced symbols:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| $\circ$ | Coexistence of active prohibitions without their reduction to each other |

$\odot$ does not link elements or introduce relationships between them. It fixes the impossibility of going beyond the limit.

> **An axis arise if and only if permission R is maintained by the $\odot$ operation as a reproducible mode.**

$Axis_D \equiv \odot R^D$  
$Axis_F \equiv \odot R^F$  
$Axis_C \equiv \odot R^C$

> **An axis is a stabilized permission.**

Important:

- $+$ and $-$ **are not** values, states, or evaluations;

> **Note on notation.** In the subsequent text, instead of the signs $+$ and $-$, we use the literal statuses $a$ (active — realization) and $l$ (limit — boundary). > **A cycle is the minimal permissible closedness of distinction in projection.**

> **A cycle is maintained not by a connection of elements, but by a system of prohibitions.**

In total, there are three such cycles — according to the number of ways to choose the $\odot$-axis:

## 6. The Octahedron as a Representational Structure

After the derivation of 6 poles and 8 contextual configurations was completed in §5, the question arises:

**What is the minimal connected geometric form in which these elements can coexist without violating any prohibition?**

It is important to emphasize: we are looking for a **representation** — a map of permissible positions on which the architecture of distinction acquires geometric readability.

### 6.1. Requirements for Representation

From §1–§5, the following rigid conditions follow:
- the three axes must be orthogonal (irreducible to each other);
- each axis has two poles ($a$ and $l$);
- antipodes must be separated (prohibition of coincidence, $FC^D$);
- the central point — where all three axes "intersect" — is not permissible (prohibition of external grounding, $FC^C$);
- the structure must be finite and closed (prohibition of tracelessness, $FC^F$).

### 6.2. Why exactly the Octahedron?

The minimal geometric structure satisfying all five conditions is the **octahedron**.

The octahedron is the only regular polyhedron in which:
- there are exactly three axes;
- each has exactly two antipodal poles;
- all poles have the same degree (4);
- the center is not a permissible node;
- the structure is closed and finite.

The octahedron was not chosen for beauty — it is **necessitated** as the minimal topological representation.

### 6.3. Why not another figure?

The carrier cannot be a **cube**: a cube has 8 vertices and 4 main diagonals — it is a permissible extended vector graph (dual to the octahedron's faces), but not minimal for the basis of three primary axes.
The carrier cannot be a **tetrahedron**: it does not contain antipodal pairs or orthogonality.

### 6.4. Vertices, Edges, and Faces

As a canonical representation, the octahedron contains:

| Element | Number | What it is |
|---------|--------|------------|
| Vertices | 6 | 6 absolute poles (2 per axis from §3.5) |
| Edges | 12 | 12 transition paths (instantiations of the $\partial$ operator) between non-antipodal poles |
| Faces | 8 | 8 contextual configurations (one projection from each axis from §5) |

These numbers are linked by Euler's formula: $V - E + F = 2 \to 6 - 12 + 8 = 2$.

Vertices localize (poles). Edges transfer tension (acts). Faces maintain semantic contexts (cells).

Of the eight faces:
- 6 are dynamic (working mixed);
- 2 are blind horizons $(a,a,a)$ and $(l,l,l)$. They fix absolute limits and do not participate in dynamic traversal.

### 6.5. Center-Prohibition

There is no geometric center in the architecture — only a **center-prohibition**: a node of impossibility that doesn't belong to any of the axes.

### 6.6. From Representation to Operator

Up to this point, the octahedron is only a **map**: a static representation of permissible configurations of distinction. Positions and links are marked on the map, but no one traverses it yet.

However, this map has a property that makes it something more than just a representation.

Each pole on the octahedron has exactly **four** non-antipodal neighbors. This means that from any permissible position, exactly four permissible transitions exist. The map has no dead ends, no degree asymmetries, and no privileged positions.

Such an equal-degree structure does not need an external triggering mechanism. If distinction once finds itself in a permissible position, it **cannot fail to have** routes of departure — they are built into the geometry itself.

This transition — from a map to something that *traverses itself* — is the transition from representation to the **operator**. But the operator, its acts, and its routes are the subject of Part II.

## 7. Pre-machine Root: The Triad of Primal Bases

Before launching the octahedron as a machine, it is necessary to fix what exactly it projects. The octahedron (§6 of Part I) is already the first full representation, but it does not arise from nowhere.

At the foundation of DOT lies a pre-machine triadic root. The world does not consist of things, but of maintained distinctions. And these distinctions rely on three primal acts:

- **Separate** (establish a boundary)
- **Manifest** (distribute the explicit and the hidden)
- **Actualize** (set in motion)

Individually, they are not maintained: a boundary without manifestation is empty, manifestation without a boundary is blurred, and action without the first two has no carrier. Only their Borromean linkage yields what we call the *machine*.

### 7.1. The Triad: Boundary / Meaning / Action

This triplet of primal acts can be fixed as three ontological primal bases. These are not yet the poles of the octahedron (there will be six of them). They are that *from which* the octahedron unfolds. To maintain the theory's rigorous law ("nothing is introduced from outside"), it is necessary to explicitly fix: the Triad is the positive semantic translation of the three negative prohibitions from Part I.

1. **Boundary (Form/Boundary)**  
   The consequence of $FC^D$ (prohibition of coincidence). That which has no right to merge into identity is logically presented as a Boundary. This is the act of separation that makes "this" and "not-this" possible (in early DOT terminology: **Tamas**).
2. **Meaning/Idea/Trace (Idea/Manifestation)**  
   The consequence of $FC^F$ (prohibition of non-fixability). That which has no right to disappear without a trace is presented as the very maintenance of the manifest and the hidden. This is the act of highlighting what has become readable and what has gone into shadow (in early DOT terminology: **Sattva**).
3. **Action (Act/Action)**  
   The consequence of $FC^C$ (prohibition of external grounding). The compulsion of distinction to rely only on itself generates self-actualization. This is the transfer, the step, the path along which the system moves from the potential to the unfolded (in early DOT terminology: **Rajas**).

Thus, the "negative foundation of algebra" (Part I) is firmly fused with the "semantics of the machine" (Part II), and the pre-machine triad Tamas/Sattva/Rajas acquires a rigorous structural basis.

### 7.2. The Principle of Projection

An important epistemic fact of DOT: **we never describe the world from the outside.** We are always already within some projection.

Therefore, the raw *carrier* of distinction (our octahedron with its 6 vertices, 12 edges, and 8 faces) cannot be seen with an "absolute gaze." We will read it through the first and most fundamental projection — the **Machine projection (Machine Family)**, where this primal basis is polarized into 6 local poles.

For this reading to begin, the machine must produce an unfolding of form. This unfolding does not occur instantaneously, but along a rigorous ladder of acts.

## 8. The Ladder of Acts: From Identity to Locality

The machine arises not from a single principle, but from a ladder of transitions: from the zero pre-distinguished level to the first full structure ($1 \to 1|2 \to 2|3 \to 6$).

### 8.1. The Whole before Distinction: $1$

DOT begins not with a world object, but with self-identity (anchor).  
$1$ is the primary invariant of self-consistency.

At this level, form and action are not yet separated. The machine is not yet launched; there is nothing to distinguish.  
But without self-identity, it is impossible to draw a boundary and understand what exactly will be maintained in the machine cycle as one and the same.

From this arises the most important pre-linguistic law of DOT:  
**A part is a mode of access to the whole, rather than a fragment of the whole.**

- $1/n$ is the minimal available representation of the whole in a given frame (the way it is forced to appear).
- $n$ is the inverse reading of the same whole as a self-identical form.

At this same minimal numeric slice, a neighboring boundary-law arises:

\[
\frac{n-1}{n}=1-\frac1n.
\]

It yields the simplest numeric readout of any boundary:

\[
\mathcal B(n)=\left(\text{body}=n-1,\ \text{carrier}=n,\ \text{debt}=\frac1n\right).
\]

Namely:

- the `carrier` is set by the number $n$;
- the `body` is read as almost complete filling $n-1$;
- the `debt` is the minimal hidden remainder $1/n$.

Hence:

- $2/3 = 1 - 1/3$ — triadic manifestation with a hidden share;
- $3/4 = 1 - 1/4$ — fourfold readable frame with a witness-slot.

This does not replace machine syntax but provides its simplest numeric slice.

### 8.2. Establishment of the Boundary (Cut): $1|2$

The first true act of the machine. The unified is sliced by a two-sided boundary.

It:
- draws the boundary (`this / not-this`);
- establishes **two sides** as mutually defined;
- creates an involution (flipping the frame reverses the sides but leaves the boundary unchanged).

$1|2$ makes the world distinguishable (locally readable), introduces two-sidedness, but does not yet yield a directed manifestation of the world. A pair (Inside/Outside) arises.

### 8.3. Distribution of Manifestation (Split): $2|3$

The second act of the machine. For distinction to become a full-fledged object of reading, a triadic split arises.

It:
- gives the first true direction to manifestation;
- maintains two shares in focus (manifest) and pushes one into the hidden (hidden share);
- makes manifestation inherently incomplete.

Here, a DOT law first appears: **any local readability leaves a hidden share.**  
$2|3$ creates asymmetry and shadow, but without the two-sided boundaries of $1|2$, this shadow could not be stably maintained.

### 8.4. The First Full Register: $2 \times 3 = 6$

As soon as the two-sided boundary operator ($1|2$) is applied to each of the three independent directions of manifestation ($2|3$), the three primal acts are polarized, receiving two ends (poles) each.

This is the combinatorial limit: $2 \times 3 = 6$.  
Two sides are applied to the triad of primal bases, yielding the *Machine Six*. This is the first level where a stable machine object appears, rather than just the conditions for its possibility.

## 13. Artifacts: What remains after the machine read-out

Only after the assembly of topology and optics does a layer of phenomena arise.

### 13.1. Frame and the Arrow of Time

`Frame` is a *context-cell* selected by a local aperture as the current world.

A *context-cell* itself is not yet a *frame*. It becomes a *frame* only when optics (§12) selects it as the point of application for the current reading. Before that, all eight cells are equal.

**Discovery of the Arrow of Time:** It is here that time arises. Time is not an external parameter, but a *sequence of witness-frame switchings*. When optics fixes one *context-cell* as a *frame*, the cell conjugate to it goes into shadow (*shadow*). The next step of the machine is a new selection of a contextual cell. Thus, the arrow of time is directed toward increasing the number of read frame-switchings.

### 13.2. Shadow

`Shadow` is the conjugate *shadow-cell* opposite to the frame.

The shadow is not an emptiness but a hidden structural complement to the *frame*.

Shadow is not a lost remainder or a projection error. It is a conjugate cell that contains exactly those poles that the *frame* did not select. Without a shadow, a *frame* has no defined boundary: it is the presence of the conjugate complement that makes the frame finite and closed.

### 13.3. Tail

The tail does not lie at the foundation.  
It arises when the partial local *optics* fails to read the *topology* without loss.

It can be read in two compatible ways:

1. **topology/optics debt**  
   the local aperture loses part of the full structure;

2. **face-pair tension**  
   an uncollapsed tension remains between the selected *context-cell* and its conjugate *shadow-cell*.

The tail is the observable weight of the unremoved shadow. Taking *face-pair tension* into account, this irreducible residual structure can be canonically described as a **low-rank tensor**, where the diagonal acts as *direct response*, and the off-diagonal elements as *resonant tension* between conjugate faces.

The tail is not an error or a random discrepancy. It is structural: it arises every time a local aperture attempts to read the machine, but the completeness of the topology does not fit into the reading frame. The tail is always present as long as the reading remains a projection.

### 13.4. Object

An object is not a point.  
And it is not just a *carrier* of poles.

An object is:

**the self-consistent closure of a traversal**,  
locally consistent with its shadow and its tail.

This is no longer a primitive but the result of a successful closure.

An object is not a point in space and not a ready-made thing. It is a closed path, consistent with its shadow and having a certain tail. An object arises when a traversal returns to the initial position without destroying either the *frame* or the *shadow*.

### 13.5. Interaction

Interaction does not reside within a single bare edge.  
It begins where:

- two *register-bodies* meet;
- their *frame/shadow* geometry overlaps;
- the total tail is redistributed.

That is, interaction lives not on an *isolated edge*, but on a *cross-register overlap*.
