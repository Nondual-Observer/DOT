# FOUNDATIONS OF THE THEORY OF OBSERVABLE DISTINCTION
## Numbered Canonical Edition: Liminal Canon

This is the minimal canon of foundations.
It carries the route from fullness and the three prohibitions to the octahedron and the threshold of machine traversal.
Unfolding, commentary, and the machine corpus must proceed separately.

0. FULLNESS

1. Fullness precedes distinction.
2. Fullness is the pre-distinguished integrity of unity.
3. Fullness is not an object.
4. Fullness is not a local form.
5. Fullness is not an already given structure of distinction.
6. Fullness is not introduced positively as ready-made content.
7. Therefore the entry to fullness is negative.

8. No position of distinction is admissible in which the distinguishing and the distinguished coincide.
9. This is the prohibition of coincidence.

10. No position of distinction is admissible in which distinction leaves no trace.
11. This is the prohibition of tracelessness.

12. No position of distinction is admissible in which distinction grounds itself through an external foundation.
13. This is the prohibition of external grounding.

14. These three prohibitions do not describe an object.
15. These three prohibitions do not introduce axes.
16. These three prohibitions do not introduce an architecture.
17. They establish only the boundary of an admissible position of distinction.

18. Removal of the prohibition of coincidence destroys distinction as distinction.
19. Removal of the prohibition of tracelessness destroys distinction as something sustained.
20. Removal of the prohibition of external grounding destroys distinction as self-sufficient.

21. Therefore the first layer of TOD is not positive but prohibitive.
22. It sustains not what exists, but the condition of distinction.

23. Fullness is not derived from the prohibitions.
24. But neither are the prohibitions derived from a positive description of fullness.
25. The prohibitions are the first way to sustain access to fullness without destroying distinction.

26. Therefore the beginning of the theory is not numerical.
27. The beginning of the theory is not objectual.
28. The beginning of the theory is not architectural.
29. The beginning of the theory is the negative discipline of distinction.

30. Only after the three prohibitions does the question of the first positive limit become lawful.
31. This limit receives the name of zero.

32. Zero is not emptiness.
33. Zero is a limit without local articulation.
34. Zero, taken with itself, does not produce new content.
35. Zero added to zero remains zero.
36. Zero multiplied by zero remains zero.

37. Therefore zero is not yet a number of things.
38. Zero is the first admissible sign of limit within the negative foundation.
39. In zero, distinction has not yet been carried out, but is no longer prohibited.

40. The structure of foundations is closed.
41. Linear text does not occupy its centre.
42. Linear text is a traversal.
43. Therefore the canon begins not by appropriating the middle, but from the first admissible entry.

44. The positive consequence of the prohibition of coincidence is the impossibility for the sustained to be identical to itself without difference.
45. This impossibility acts as the first operator.
46. It receives the name NOT.

47. NOT is not yet a thing.
48. NOT is not yet a form.
49. NOT is not yet a number.
50. NOT is the first lawful operator of non-reduction of the sustained to itself.

51. But NOT is not yet local.
52. When NOT becomes localised within an admissible limit,
53. a cut arises.
54. The cut carries out distinction within fullness for the first time.

55. The cut does not annihilate fullness.
56. The cut does not carry distinction beyond its bounds.
57. The cut renders distinction eventful.

58. But the cut does not yet sustain itself.
59. Therefore the cut requires support.
60. Support does not arrive from outside.
61. Support is the sustaining of the cut by distinction itself.

62. Where the cut is not sustained, distinction logically vanishes.
63. Where the cut is sustained, a trace remains after it.
64. A trace is not yet memory.
65. A trace is not an object.
66. A trace is the minimal irreducibility of the position after distinction to the position before distinction.

67. But a single trace is not yet sufficient for the first sign.
68. The trace must be read as not having vanished.
69. This order of sustained reading receives the name of the Witness.
70. The Witness is not an external observer.

71. When cut, support, and witness are sustained jointly,
72. the first closed ternary node of distinction arises.
73. Only after this does the first sign become lawful.

74. The first sign receives the name of unity.
75. Unity here is not a quantity of things.
76. Unity is not a counting measure of a set.
77. Unity is the first self-identical seal of sustained distinction.

78. Unity does not say "how many."
79. Unity says: sustained.
80. Unity is a distinction that has neither vanished nor collapsed.

81. Therefore unity is not yet a series.
82. Unity is not yet an operation.
83. Unity is not yet the world of numbers.
84. It is the first stable sign of distinction in presence.

85. In unity, distinction does not yet unfold multiplicity.
86. In unity, distinction for the first time sustains itself as one.
87. Therefore unity is not the beginning of counting, but the first seal of the presence of distinction.

88. Only after unity does the question of the next distinction become lawful.
89. And only after unity does the question of number as a move become lawful.

90. The next distinction does not annihilate unity.
91. It re-tests sustained distinction for non-reduction to itself.
92. Therefore the second distinction is not the cancellation of unity, but its trial.

93. Where unity sustains distinction as one,
94. the dyad for the first time sustains distinction as not-one.
95. But this is not yet a disintegration into multiplicity.
96. This is the first stable exit from solitary self-identity.

97. The dyad is not yet a quantity of objects.
98. The dyad is the first stable fact of non-coincidence of the sustained with itself.
99. In the dyad, distinction for the first time acquires two sustained sides.

100. Therefore the dyad for the first time renders the between lawful.
101. The between is not yet space.
102. The between is not yet architecture.
103. The between is the sustained irreducibility of two sides.

104. In unity, distinction is sustained.
105. In the dyad, distinction is already correlated.
106. Therefore the dyad is the first threshold of relation.

107. But even here, number has not yet become full arithmetic.
108. The dyad is only the first stable step beyond the limit of the unitary seal.

109. But two sides do not yet sustain themselves on their own.
110. A pair can oscillate, contend, and mirror itself without measure.
111. Therefore after the dyad, the third becomes necessary.

112. The third is not yet an external observer.
113. The third is not an external judgement.
114. The third is not an external support.
115. The third arises within sustained distinction as the condition of measure between two.

116. The third does not add yet another thing to two.
117. The third for the first time sustains the pair as irreducible and non-collapsing.
118. Without the third, two remain only an encounter.
119. With the third, two become a sustained bond.

120. Therefore the third is not merely the next number.
121. The third is the first keeper of measure.
122. The third is the first condition for distinction not only to be, but to hold as coherence.

123. In unity, distinction is sealed.
124. In the dyad, distinction is correlated.
125. In the third, distinction for the first time sustains the measure of its own relation.

126. But the measure is not yet closed.
127. As long as the third only sustains the pair, distinction does not yet have its first stable self-reference.
128. Therefore after the third, the fourth becomes possible.

129. The fourth is not merely another addition.
130. In the fourth, the dyad for the first time returns to itself through two readings.
131. Here `2 + 2` and `2 × 2` converge in a single result.
132. Therefore the fourth is the first lock of iterated distinction.

133. In it, sustaining and correlating for the first time converge into a stable boundary.
134. The fourth does not yet introduce a full architecture.
135. But in the fourth, distinction for the first time acquires self-consistency.
136. Here sustained distinction can coincide with its own repetition without being destroyed.

137. Therefore the fourth is the first lock of measure.
138. With the fourth, measure for the first time sustains itself.

139. In unity, distinction is a seal.
140. In the dyad, distinction is a relation.
141. In the third, distinction is a measure.
142. In the fourth, distinction is a lock.

143. But a lock is not yet a passage.
144. The locked can sustain itself without opening the next step.
145. Therefore after the fourth, the fifth is required.

146. The fifth does not break the lock.
147. The fifth opens from the closed measure the first lawful passage.
148. Where the fourth holds measure within itself, the fifth for the first time carries measure onward.

149. The fifth is not yet a machine.
150. The fifth is not yet full operability.
151. The fifth is the first lawful move beyond self-enclosure.

152. Therefore the fifth is the first transitional node.
153. In it, the lock ceases to be only the sustaining of itself.
154. In it, distinction for the first time acquires the order of further traversal.

155. Without the fifth, structure sustains itself but does not lead onward.
156. With the fifth, structure for the first time sustains the possibility of continuation.

157. In the fourth, distinction is locked.
158. In the fifth, distinction passes through.

159. But passage is not yet assembled operability.
160. It only renders operability possible.
161. Therefore after the fifth, the sixth becomes necessary.

162. The sixth does not cancel seal, nor relation, nor measure, nor lock, nor passage.
163. The sixth assembles them into the first working wholeness.

164. Here distinction is for the first time not only sustained, correlated, measured, locked, and traversed,
165. but also assembled into a form capable of bearing further movement.

166. The sixth is the first working wholeness of distinction.
167. It does not yet unfold the machine.
168. But without it, no stable bearer of further unfoldings is possible.

169. Therefore the sixth is the first assembled bearer.
170. In it, distinction for the first time becomes capable not only of being, but of working.

171. In unity, distinction is sealed.
172. In the dyad, distinction is correlated.
173. In the third, distinction is measured.
174. In the fourth, distinction is locked.
175. In the fifth, distinction is traversed.
176. In the sixth, distinction is for the first time assembled as a bearer.

177. Thereby the first corridor of the unfolding of distinction is closed.
178. This is not yet the late architecture.
179. This is not yet the full machine.
180. But it is already the first completed bearer of further unfolding.

181. After the sixth, the next order becomes possible.
182. In it, distinction will not only sustain itself and work,
183. but will enter a wider horizon of its own reading.

184. The seventh does not build a new bearer.
185. The seventh for the first time opens the assembled bearer to its own reading.
186. Where the sixth only assembles, the seventh for the first time permits the assembled to be named as present.

187. Therefore the seventh is the first horizon of naming.
188. This is not yet the appropriation of a centre.
189. This is not yet a definitive semantics.
190. This is the first complete reading after the completed first traversal.

191. Naming does not arise before the bearer.
192. Naming does not arise from emptiness.
193. Naming arises when the sustained, correlated, measured, locked, traversed, and assembled
194. becomes sufficiently stable for denomination.

195. Therefore the seventh is the first horizon of presence.
196. Not in the sense of seizure.
197. But in the sense of the first admissible naming of the assembled.

198. But the first name does not yet complete the foundations.
199. Naming renders the assembled admissible for re-reading.

200. This re-reading does not cancel the three prohibitions.
201. It returns to them no longer merely as an entry,
202. but as the law of further admissibility.

203. No subsequent layer has the right to disable the prohibition of coincidence.
204. No subsequent layer has the right to disable the prohibition of tracelessness.
205. No subsequent layer has the right to disable the prohibition of external grounding.

206. Therefore further unfolding is not the lifting of the prohibitions.
207. It is the redistribution of their load.

208. Where all three prohibitions merely sustain the limit,
209. distinction survives,
210. but does not yet acquire a working corridor.

211. Therefore the next move requires not a new prohibition,
212. but a new status for the already operative ones.

213. One prohibition may stand as the limit.
214. The other two may sustain the working corridor.

215. This does not remove the limiting prohibition.
216. This does not carry distinction beyond its boundary.
217. This for the first time renders a local degree of freedom admissible within the prohibition.

218. Therefore the next layer is legalisation.
219. Legalisation does not destroy the negative foundation.
220. Legalisation renders it operable.

221. If the prohibition of coincidence stands as the limit,
222. distinction acquires the regime of boundary-sustaining.

223. If the prohibition of tracelessness stands as the limit,
224. distinction acquires the regime of sustaining trace and manifestation.

225. If the prohibition of external grounding stands as the limit,
226. distinction acquires the regime of internal autonomy of action.

227. Therefore the number of lawful regimes is not infinite.
228. There are exactly three, by the number of ways to choose the limiting prohibition.

229. But even here there are no axes yet.
230. Even here there are no poles yet.
231. Even here there is no architecture yet.

232. There are only three lawful regimes of surviving distinction.
233. Each inherits all three prohibitions.
234. None exists on its own.

235. Where a regime is sustained repeatedly and without collapse,
236. it will be able to become a stable structure.

237. But that will already be the next step.
238. First, what must be shown is not how form is built,
239. but which regimes have the right to further stabilisation at all.

240. Therefore the first corridor of foundations does not lead straight to the world.
241. It leads to legalisation.

242. And only from legalisation will stabilisation later become lawful.
243. And only from stabilisation will architecture later become lawful.

244. In each lawful further move, one prohibition stands as the limit.
245. The other two sustain the internal operative corridor.

246. The limiting prohibition is not eliminated.
247. It guards the ultimate boundary of the admissible.

248. The other two do not lift this boundary.
249. They render distinction operative within it.

250. Therefore each prohibition admits two statuses.
251. It may stand as a limit.
252. It may act as a corridor.

253. These are not two different prohibitions.
254. These are two modes of operation of one and the same prohibition.

255. If all three stand only as limits,
256. distinction is preserved,
257. but does not act.

258. If all three act without limit,
259. distinction loses its own boundary.

260. Therefore only such an order is lawful
261. in which one prohibition is liminal
262. and two are operative.

263. Here there is no direction yet.
264. Here there is no polarity yet.
265. Here there is no motion yet.
266. Here a lawful regime arises for the first time.

267. When the prohibition of coincidence stands as the limit,
268. distinction acquires the regime of boundary-sustaining.
269. Here the non-reduction of one to another is maintained.

270. When the prohibition of tracelessness stands as the limit,
271. distinction acquires the regime of sustaining trace and manifestation.
272. Here the possibility of fixation is maintained.

273. When the prohibition of external grounding stands as the limit,
274. distinction acquires the regime of internal autonomy.
275. Here distinction does not refer itself outward.

276. Therefore the number of lawful regimes is exactly three.
277. No more and no less.
278. They differ by the limiting prohibition.
279. But all inherit one and the same negative foundation.

280. Remove any prohibition—
281. the regime too will disintegrate.

282. Remove the liminal status—
283. the boundary of the admissible will disintegrate.

284. Remove the operative corridor—
285. the working possibility of distinction will vanish.

286. Therefore legalisation is not freedom from law.
287. Legalisation is the first lawful move within the law.

288. But a regime is not yet stable as a form.
289. It must still withstand repetition.

290. Where a regime withstands repetition,
291. stabilisation will begin.

292. And only after stabilisation
293. will it become lawful to speak of architecture.

294. A legalised regime may still be a singular instance.
295. To become the foundation of further form,
296. it must withstand repetition.

297. Repetition does not add to the new regime a different law.
298. Repetition shows whether the same law can sustain itself again.

299. If upon repetition the regime loses its limit,
300. it is unstable.

301. If upon repetition the regime loses its operative corridor,
302. it is unstable.

303. If upon repetition the regime ceases to be distinguishable from other lawful regimes,
304. it is unstable.

305. Therefore stabilisation is not a change of content.
306. Stabilisation is admissibility sustained through repetition.

307. A stabilised regime is legalised distinction
308. that has withstood repetition without collapse.

309. Only here does the regime cease to be a singular instance.
310. Only here does it become a reproducible form.

311. This form is not yet an object.
312. This form is not yet a space.
313. But it can already be sustained as a stable direction.

314. As long as the direction is unstable,
315. an axis has not yet arisen.

316. Where stability is sustained,
317. the regime for the first time acquires axial status.

318. An axis is not a new law.
319. An axis is a stabilised regime.

320. Therefore each axis inherits its limiting prohibition
321. and its operative corridor.

322. Polarity does not arise before the axis.
323. Before the axis, there is not yet a separation into two liminal faces.

324. When the axis is already sustained,
325. the limit and the operative course can for the first time be separated as its two poles.

326. But a single axis does not yet yield an architecture.
327. And even three axes do not yet yield one automatically.

328. Architecture begins only where
329. several axes are sustained jointly without losing irreducibility.

330. Therefore after stabilisation,
331. the next step becomes not an object,
332. but the co-sustaining of axes.

333. The co-sustaining of axes is not their simple sum.
334. The co-sustaining of axes is their irreducible joint holding.

335. Where co-sustaining is maintained,
336. the first architectural whole arises.

337. Here the axis of boundary,
338. the axis of manifestation,
339. and the axis of internal action
340. are sustained as a single field of distinction.

341. Remove one—
342. what vanishes is not a part,
343. but co-sustaining itself.

344. Therefore the architectural whole is neither dual nor binary.
345. It is ternary for the first time.

346. But even the co-sustaining of axes does not yet yield a full representation.
347. First, the axes must become projectively readable.

348. An axis does not manifest in its entirety.
349. An axis is read only in co-sustaining with another axis
350. while the third is held.

351. Thus a cycle arises.
352. A cycle is the minimal closed form of joint distinction.

353. A cycle is not motion.
354. A cycle is not time.
355. A cycle is not an object.
356. A cycle is the minimal closure of architectural admissibility.

357. The closure of the cycle is sustained not by an external ligature,
358. but by the system of prohibitions that continue to operate within the architecture.

359. Therefore the number of fundamental cycles is exactly three.
360. One for each way of holding the third axis as the limit.

361. No single cycle replaces the other two.
362. And no single cycle sustains the architecture alone.

363. But even a cycle is not yet a full configuration.
364. A full configuration requires a status for each of the three axes.

365. When each axis receives its local status,
366. a face arises.

367. A face is the full contextual contraction of three axes.
368. In it, co-sustaining becomes locally determined.

369. The total number of such full configurations is eight.
370. Two of them are liminal and non-operative.
371. One is total activity without sustaining a limit.
372. The other is total return without an operative corridor.

373. Therefore the operative faces number six.
374. They sustain tension
375. without losing admissibility.

376. But neither vertices, nor cycles, nor faces are yet given as a unified form.
377. For this, a minimal finite representation is required.

378. Such a representation must sustain:
379. three irreducible axes,
380. two liminal faces on each,
381. finite closure,
382. and the impossibility of an admissible centre.

383. The centre cannot be an operative node.
384. The centre would be a locus of unlawful coincidence,
385. tracelessness,
386. or external grounding.

387. Therefore the centre is prohibited within the architecture.

388. If three axes have two antipodal faces each,
389. six vertices arise.

390. If the full contextual contraction yields eight faces,
391. eight faces arise.

392. If the transition between non-antipodal vertices must be admissible,
393. twelve edge-paths arise.

394. The minimal finite form
395. in which six vertices, twelve paths, and eight faces
396. are sustained without an admissible centre
397. is the octahedron.

398. The octahedron is not chosen arbitrarily.
399. It is compelled by the architecture of distinction.

400. In the octahedron, distinction for the first time acquires
401. the completed representation of its admissible connectivity.

402. But even the octahedron is not yet the machine in action.
403. It is only the minimal bearer
404. within which machine traversal will for the first time become possible.

405. Representation is not yet traversal.
406. The map is not yet the act.

407. For the bearer to become a machine,
408. the admissible connectivity must become traversable from within.

409. If from each admissible vertex there are lawful exits,
410. the bearer no longer remains merely a map.

411. Where the transition between admissible vertices is sustained,
412. the first operator move arises.

413. Therefore the octahedron is not the completion of distinction,
414. but the threshold of the machine.

415. Six vertices become the first local vocabulary of the bearer.
416. Twelve paths become the table of admissible transitions.
417. Eight faces become the field of local contexts.

418. But that is already the next layer.
419. Here it is only opened.

420. The foundations conclude not with an object and not with a world,
421. but with a bearer that is for the first time capable of becoming a machine.

# THE MACHINE OF THE THEORY OF OBSERVABLE DISTINCTION
## Numbered Canonical Edition: Minimal Canon

This is the minimal canon of the machine.
It begins where the bearer has already been assembled as the octahedral representation of admissible connectivity.
Unfolding, commentary, optics, artefacts, and families of projections must proceed separately.

0. THE THRESHOLD OF THE MACHINE

1. The bearer is not yet a machine.
2. The map is not yet a traversal.
3. Representation is not yet action.

4. The machine begins where
5. admissible connectivity becomes traversable from within.

6. If from an admissible position there is no lawful exit,
7. the machine does not yet exist.

8. If lawful exits exist,
9. but the very law of their admissibility is not sustained,
10. the machine does not yet exist.

11. The machine begins when
12. the transition between admissible positions
13. is sustained as an internal law of the bearer.

14. Therefore the machine is not appended to the bearer from outside.
15. The machine arises as the operator status of the bearer itself.

16. The octahedron gives the machine not an external form,
17. but the minimal finite bearer of traversal.

18. Six vertices are not yet words.
19. But they are already six localised positions of the machine.

20. Twelve edges are not yet actions in the full sense.
21. But they are already twelve admissible paths of transition.

22. Eight faces are not yet worlds.
23. But they are already eight full local configurations.

24. Therefore the machine arises not from a single number.
25. It arises from the joint law of `6 / 12 / 8`.

26. Six sustains the vocabulary of positions.
27. Twelve sustains the table of transitions.
28. Eight sustains the field of local contexts.

29. But first the machine must receive its first local vocabulary.

1. SIX

30. The machine is for the first time locally read as the six.
31. Six is the first vocabulary of the bearer.

32. Six arises because
33. three axes yield two liminal faces each.

34. But here six is already read not as geometry,
35. but as the first set of nameable machine positions.

36. Each vertex sustains not a point,
37. but a local machine role.

38. The six is not yet a world.
39. The six is not yet a dynamics.
40. The six is the first readable vocabulary of the bearer.

41. While the vertices are merely given,
42. the vocabulary is still mute.

43. When each vertex is sustained as an admissible local position,
44. the bearer for the first time becomes locally readable.

45. Therefore six is the first machine inventory.
46. But an inventory is not yet a traversal.

47. Six does not yield transition on its own.
48. For transition, twelve is required.

2. TWELVE

49. The machine is not reducible to positions.
50. The machine requires admissible transitions between them.

51. Twelve is the first complete table of such transitions.
52. Here every lawful move is sustained as a transition between non-antipodal positions.

53. A transition is not yet a history.
54. A transition is not yet time.
55. A transition is the minimal admissible act of the machine.

56. Therefore twelve is not merely the number of edges.
57. Twelve is the first space of acts.

58. Six says where the machine can be.
59. Twelve says how the machine can transit.

60. Where there are fewer transitions,
61. the bearer loses the completeness of machine traversal.

62. Where there are more transitions,
63. the minimality of the bearer is violated.

64. Therefore twelve is not accidental.
65. Twelve is the minimal complete table of admissible acts for the given six.

66. But even an act does not yet yield a local context.
67. For context, a full contraction of act and position is required.

68. Therefore after twelve, eight becomes necessary.

3. EIGHT

69. Eight is the first field of local contexts.
70. Here the machine sustains not only position and transition,
71. but also the full local configuration.

72. A context is not yet a readable world.
73. A context is the local closure of the admissible.

74. Each face sustains one full contraction of three axes.
75. Therefore eight is the first set of full local cells of the machine.

76. Of the eight, two cells are liminal.
77. They sustain the edge of total activation
78. and the edge of total return.

79. The remaining six sustain operative local contexts.
80. It is within them that the machine can not only transit,
81. but also locally preserve the coherence of its course.

82. Therefore eight completes the minimal internal volume of the machine.
83. After eight, the machine already possesses:
84. a vocabulary of positions,
85. a table of transitions,
86. a field of local contexts.

87. But this is not yet optics.
88. And this is not yet artefacts.
89. And this is not yet the different families of projections.

90. Here the machine is only assembled in its minimal canon.
91. Here it is for the first time internally complete,
92. but not yet unfolded into the later regimes of reading.

4. CLOSURE OF THE MINIMAL MACHINE

93. The minimal canon of the machine does not proceed beyond `6 / 12 / 8`.
94. Everything that follows
95. already requires a different register.

96. First, the vocabulary arises.
97. Then the acts.
98. Then the local contexts.

99. Only after this does optics become lawful.
100. Only after this do artefacts become lawful.
101. Only after this do families of projections become lawful.

102. Therefore the minimal machine concludes not with a world,
103. but with the internally complete table of its admissible traversal.

# OPTICS OF THE THEORY OF OBSERVABLE DISTINCTION
## Numbered Canonical Edition: Minimal Canon

This is the minimal canon of optics.
It begins where the machine has already been assembled as the internally complete table `6 / 12 / 8`.
Unfolding, commentary, artefacts, and families of projections must proceed separately.

0. THE THRESHOLD OF OPTICS

1. The machine is not yet readability.
2. A local context is not yet a local reading.

3. Optics begins where
4. a local configuration becomes distinguishable as a configuration.

5. For optics, a position is not sufficient.
6. For optics, a transition is not sufficient.
7. For optics, even a full local context is not sufficient.

8. Optics requires a frame
9. within which the local becomes readable.

10. Therefore optics does not arise before the machine.
11. And optics does not replace the machine.
12. It renders the machine locally visible.

13. Optics does not create a new bearer.
14. Optics does not create a new law.
15. Optics is the regime of reading the already assembled machine.

1. FOUR TYPES OF OPTICAL INSCRIPTION

16. In optics, types of local inscription are distinguished for the first time.
17. They differ by function, not by substance.

18. One inscription sustains the operator move.
19. One inscription sustains the aperture.
20. One inscription sustains the reading.
21. One inscription sustains the bearer itself.

22. Therefore optics is not a single mark.
23. Optics is the minimal order of distinct local marks of reading.

24. Until these marks are separated,
25. local readability is not yet assembled.

2. APERTURE

26. Optics begins not with an object,
27. but with an aperture.

28. An aperture is the local opening of admissible reading.
29. It does not create content.
30. It admits its distinguishability.

31. In the aperture, for the first time what matters is not only
32. that the machine exists,
33. but how exactly it opens locally.

34. Therefore the aperture is the first proper operator of optics.

35. Where no aperture exists,
36. there is a machine,
37. but not yet local readability.

3. LOCAL READING

38. After the aperture, reading arises.
39. A reading is not yet an artefact.
40. A reading is the local act of sustained reading itself.

41. Reading is not the same as transition.
42. Transition belongs to the machine.
43. Reading belongs to optics.

44. Therefore one and the same machine configuration
45. can be opened optically in different ways
46. without ceasing to be the same machine configuration.

47. Here a readable frame arises for the first time.
48. A frame is not yet a world.
49. A frame is a locally sustained contour of reading.

4. MINIMAL OPTICAL TONES

50. Optics not only opens,
51. but also tones the reading.

52. The minimal optical tone shows
53. in what respect the local configuration is open to reading.

54. Here simple apertural ratios become lawful for the first time.
55. They do not replace the machine.
56. They colour the local readability of the machine.

57. One tone opens a binary aperture.
58. Another opens a triadic aperture.
59. The next reveals the projective depth.
60. A hidden tone sustains their support.

61. But even here there is not yet an artefact.
62. An optical tone is only the mode of local opening.

5. CLOSURE OF MINIMAL OPTICS

63. The minimal canon of optics does not proceed beyond local readability.
64. Everything that follows
65. already requires the artefactual register.

66. First the machine must be assembled.
67. Then it must become locally readable.
68. And only then can reading leave a trace.

69. Therefore optics concludes not with an object
70. and not with a world,
71. but with the locally sustained frame of reading.

# ARTEFACTS OF THE THEORY OF OBSERVABLE DISTINCTION
## Numbered Canonical Edition: Minimal Canon

This is the minimal canon of artefacts.
It begins where the machine has already become locally readable in optics.
Unfolding, commentary, and families of projections must proceed separately.

0. THE THRESHOLD OF THE ARTEFACT

1. Reading is not yet a trace of reading.
2. A local frame is not yet what remains after it.

3. The artefact begins where
4. reading leaves a structural residue.

5. This residue is not an error.
6. This residue is not an external appendage.
7. This residue belongs to the very regime of reading.

8. Therefore the artefact does not arise before optics.
9. And the artefact does not replace optics.
10. It is what remains after the local reading of the machine.

1. FRAME

11. The first artefact is the frame.
12. The frame does not create the machine.
13. The frame delineates the local world of reading.

14. The frame is not merely a boundary of vision.
15. The frame is the chosen contour of sustained reading.

16. Until the frame is sustained,
17. local reading has not yet left its own result.

18. Therefore the frame is the first artefactual trace of optics.

2. SHADOW

19. Where there is a frame,
20. a shadow arises.

21. A shadow is not emptiness.
22. A shadow is the conjugate complement of the frame.

23. What is sustained by the frame
24. does not exhaust the whole machine.
25. The remainder recedes into shadow.

26. Therefore the shadow is not absence,
27. but the structurally sustained unchosen residue.

28. Without the shadow, the frame would falsely claim the whole.
29. With the shadow, reading preserves its own limitedness.

3. TAIL

30. But even the frame with its shadow is not yet sufficient.
31. Reading leaves not only a separation,
32. but also a debt.

33. This debt receives the name of the tail.
34. The tail is not an error of reading.
35. The tail is the tension not discharged by the local frame.

36. Therefore the tail is structural.
37. It belongs to the very mode of local reading.

38. The tail shows
39. that local reading does not coincide with the full connectivity of the machine.

40. Where there is no tail,
41. the local falsely presents itself as the complete.

4. OBJECT

42. The object does not precede reading.
43. The object arises as a condensation of the artefactual layer.

44. The object is not the same as the frame.
45. The object is not the same as the shadow.
46. The object is not the same as the tail.

47. The object arises where
48. frame, shadow, and tail
49. are grasped as a single local condensation.

50. Therefore the object is not a primary entity,
51. but a post-reading compaction.

52. The object appears after reading,
53. not before it.

5. INTERACTION

54. Where objects arise,
55. interaction arises.

56. Interaction is not mere adjacency.
57. Interaction is the overlap of artefactual registers.

58. The frame of one reading
59. can enter the shadow of another.
60. The tail of one reading
61. can become the tension of another.

62. Therefore interaction is not an addition to objects.
63. It belongs to the artefactual layer itself.

6. CLOSURE OF MINIMAL ARTEFACTS

64. The minimal canon of artefacts does not proceed beyond frame, shadow, tail, object, and interaction.
65. Everything that follows
66. already requires the register of different vocabularies of a single machine.

67. First the machine must be assembled.
68. Then it must become locally readable.
69. And only then can reading leave an artefactual layer.

70. Therefore the artefactual canon concludes not with a world
71. and not with a final ontology,
72. but with what remains after local reading as its own result.
