**Function of this map:** to separate what in the current chemical text can already be kept as canon-safe, what still sounds too strong, and which passages necessarily require a separate computational appendix.

---

## 1. General Assessment

The current chemical text does not look weak or accidental.
On the contrary,
it is already assembled as a strong late-stage layer:

- it has a unified line;
- it has an explicit bridge
  from particle-layer to shell-layer;
- it has the axis
  `prime modes -> shell bases -> ionization -> screening -> tails`;
- and it contains several very strong organizing claims.

The problem is not the lack of content,
but the **status of the claims**.

At present, the document often speaks
as though it were already established canon,
where in fact the level is closer to:

- strong structural correspondence;
- late-stage synthesis;
- or hypothesis backed by fit.

So the task of stabilization
is not to weaken the text,
but to **separate the levels of hardness**.

## 7. Recommended Stabilization Without Loss of Force

### Option A. Keep the text strong, but change the marking

What to do:

- `[D]` -> `[G1]` or `[G2]`
- “exactly” -> “stably / correlates strictly”
- “theorem” -> “structural law / reading / correspondence”

This is the minimal stabilization.

### Option B. Split the corpus into main text + appendix

This is the best option.

The main text:

- remains large,
  strong,
  synthetic;
- holds only the major structural claims.

The appendix:

- TOPO_MAP 118;
- methodology;
- error tables;
- screening extraction;
- occurrence tables for macro-primes;
- claim statuses.

Then the main text
does not lose its energy,
but becomes defensible.

### Option C. Introduce two statuses inside the document itself

- `Canon Core`
- `Strong Chemical Reading`

And then separate
where one stands
and where the other stands
inside each section.

---

## 8. Short Conclusion

The current chemical canon
does not look false.
It looks **fixed earlier than justified as more rigorous than it currently is**.

The strong sides of the text:

- a good overall carrier-line;
- living late-stage synthesis;
- strong organizing numbers and bases;
- good integration of tails.

The weak point is not the idea,
but the status:

- too much theorem-tone;
- too little explicit appendix-support.

The most correct stabilization is:

> **not to weaken the text,  
> but to give it a second layer in the form of a computational appendix  
> and to separate strict derivation from strong reading honestly.**
