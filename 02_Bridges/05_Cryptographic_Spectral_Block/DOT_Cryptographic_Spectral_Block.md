## 1. The Object

Fix $n \ge 3$.

We combine three ingredients:

1. the $n$-dimensional cross-polytope graph
$$
   O_n \cong K_{2,\dots,2},
$$
   with $2n$ pole vertices;
2. the $n$-dimensional hypercube graph
$$
   Q_n
$$
   with $2^n$ cube vertices;
3. a binary incidence matrix
$$
   B \in \{0,1\}^{2n \times 2^n}
$$
   coupling poles to cube vertices.

### 1.1. Pole notation

For each coordinate axis $i \in \{1,\dots,n\}$ there are two poles:

- $+e_i$,
- $-e_i$.

We index them as:

- vertex $2i$ for $+e_i$,
- vertex $2i+1$ for $-e_i$.

### 1.2. Cube vertices

Cube vertices are bit strings
$$
s \in \{0,1\}^n.
$$

### 1.3. Incidence rule

Pole $v$ with axis $i$ and sign bit $b \in \{0,1\}$ is connected to every cube vertex $s$ satisfying
$$
s_i = b.
$$

So each pole sees exactly half of the cube:
$$
2^{n-1}
$$
vertices, and each cube vertex is incident to exactly
$$
n
$$
poles.

## 2. Laplacian Form

Let:

- $m = 2n$,
- $N = 2^n$.

Let $L_{\mathrm{oct}}$ and $L_{\mathrm{cube}}$ be the Laplacians of $O_n$ and $Q_n$.

Then the Laplacian of the full block graph is:
$$
L_{\mathrm{block}} =
\begin{pmatrix}
L_{\mathrm{oct}} + 2^{n-1}I_m & -B \\
-B^T & L_{\mathrm{cube}} + nI_N
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The diagonal shifts come from the inter-graph degrees:

- every pole gains degree $2^{n-1}$ toward the cube,
- every cube vertex gains degree $n$ toward the poles.

## 3. Known Spectra of the Two Base Graphs

### 3.1. Cross-polytope graph

For
$$
O_n \cong K_{2,\dots,2},
$$
the Laplacian spectrum is:

- $\lambda = 0$ with multiplicity $1$;
- $\lambda = 2(n-1)$ with multiplicity $n$;
- $\lambda = 2n$ with multiplicity $n-1$.

The multiplicity-$n$ sector is generated by the axial vectors
$$
\phi_i,
$$
where $\phi_i$ is:

- $+1$ on $+e_i$,
- $-1$ on $-e_i$,
- $0$ elsewhere.

### 3.2. Hypercube graph

For $Q_n$, the Laplacian spectrum is:
$$
2k,\qquad k=0,\dots,n,
$$
with multiplicity
$$
\binom{n}{k}.
$$

The eigenvectors are the Walsh characters
$$
\chi_u(s)=(-1)^{\langle u,s\rangle},
$$
where
$$
u \in \{0,1\}^n,\qquad \mathrm{wt}(u)=k.
$$

## 4. The Three Structural Lemmas

These three lemmas are the real engine of the whole package.

### Lemma 1. Kernel of the incidence matrix

For every Walsh character $\chi_u$:
$$
B\chi_u = 0
\iff
\mathrm{wt}(u)\ge 2.
$$

Equivalently:
$$
\ker(B)=\mathrm{Span}\{\chi_u:\mathrm{wt}(u)\ge 2\}.
$$

#### Meaning

The incidence matrix $B$ acts as a sieve:

- it **sees** only Walsh weight $0$ and weight $1$;
- it **annihilates** all weights $2$ and above.

This is the first decisive step.

#### Proof

Fix a pole $v=(i,b)$, where $i\in\{1,\dots,n\}$ is the axis and
$b\in\{0,1\}$ is the pole sign-bit. Then
$$
(B\chi_u)_v=\sum_{s:\,s_i=b}(-1)^{\langle u,s\rangle}.
$$

Write $s=(t_1,\dots,t_n)$ with the constraint $t_i=b$. Then
$$
\langle u,s\rangle = u_i b + \sum_{j\ne i}u_j t_j
$$
modulo $2$, hence
$$
(B\chi_u)_v
=
(-1)^{u_i b}
\sum_{(t_j)_{j\ne i}\in\{0,1\}^{n-1}}
\prod_{j\ne i}(-1)^{u_j t_j}.
$$

Since the variables $t_j$ are independent, the sum factorizes:
$$
(B\chi_u)_v
=
(-1)^{u_i b}
\prod_{j\ne i}\Bigl(\sum_{t_j\in\{0,1\}}(-1)^{u_j t_j}\Bigr).
$$

Each factor is elementary:
$$
\sum_{t_j\in\{0,1\}}(-1)^{u_j t_j}
=
\begin{cases}
2,&u_j=0,\\
0,&u_j=1.
\end{cases}
$$

Therefore $(B\chi_u)_v\neq 0$ if and only if all coordinates
$u_j$ with $j\ne i$ vanish. Equivalently, $u$ has weight $0$ or $1$.
If $\mathrm{wt}(u)\ge 2$, then for every pole $v$ at least one factor is zero,
so $B\chi_u=0$.

Conversely, if $\mathrm{wt}(u)=0$, then $\chi_u=\mathbf 1_N$ and
$B\chi_u=2^{n-1}\mathbf 1_m\neq 0$.
If $\mathrm{wt}(u)=1$, say $u=e_i$, then the only nonzero rows are the two poles
on axis $i$, and
$$
(B\chi_{e_i})_{(i,0)}=2^{n-1},\qquad
(B\chi_{e_i})_{(i,1)}=-2^{n-1},
$$
so again $B\chi_{e_i}\neq 0$.

Hence
$$
\ker(B)=\mathrm{Span}\{\chi_u:\mathrm{wt}(u)\ge 2\}.
$$

---

### Lemma 2. Decoupled weight-$k$ eigenvectors

If $\mathrm{wt}(u)\ge 2$, then
$$
(0_m,\chi_u)
$$
is an eigenvector of $L_{\mathrm{block}}$ with eigenvalue
$$
2\,\mathrm{wt}(u)+n.
$$

#### Meaning

All Walsh modes of weight at least 2 survive inside the block graph as **pure cube modes**.
They do not couple back to the octahedral side because $B$ kills them.

This gives the fully decoupled sectors:
$$
D_k = \mathrm{Span}\{(0_m,\chi_u):\mathrm{wt}(u)=k\},\qquad k\ge 2.
$$

---

### Lemma 3. Exact axis–character coupling

For every axis $i$:
$$
B^T\phi_i = \chi_{e_i},
\qquad
B\chi_{e_i}=2^{n-1}\phi_i.
$$

#### Meaning

Weight-1 Walsh characters are not decoupled.
Each one couples exactly to one axial mode of the cross-polytope.

This is why the weight-1 sector must be treated differently from the higher-weight sectors.

---

### Corollary 3.1. Exact rank and singular structure of the incidence matrix

The incidence matrix $B$ has:

$$
\mathrm{rank}(B)=n+1.
$$

More precisely:

$$
\mathrm{Im}(B)=\mathrm{Span}\{\mathbf 1_m,\phi_1,\dots,\phi_n\},
$$

$$
\mathrm{Im}(B^T)=\mathrm{Span}\{\mathbf 1_N,\chi_{e_1},\dots,\chi_{e_n}\},
$$

and the nonzero eigenvalues of $BB^T$ are:

$$
n\,2^{n-1}\quad\text{with multiplicity }1,
$$

$$
2^{n-1}\quad\text{with multiplicity }n.
$$

Equivalently, the nonzero singular values of $B$ are:

$$
\sqrt{n\,2^{n-1}}\quad\text{once},
\qquad
\sqrt{2^{n-1}}\quad\text{with multiplicity }n.
$$

#### Proof

By Lemma 1,
$$
\ker(B)=\mathrm{Span}\{\chi_u:\mathrm{wt}(u)\ge 2\}.
$$
Since the Walsh basis has total dimension $2^n$, the orthogonal complement of the kernel is
$$
\mathrm{Span}\{\mathbf 1_N,\chi_{e_1},\dots,\chi_{e_n}\},
$$
which therefore coincides with $\mathrm{Im}(B^T)$. Hence
$$
\mathrm{rank}(B)=n+1.
$$

By Lemma 3,
$$
B\mathbf 1_N = 2^{n-1}\mathbf 1_m,
\qquad
B\chi_{e_i}=2^{n-1}\phi_i.
$$
So $\mathrm{Im}(B)$ is contained in
$$
\mathrm{Span}\{\mathbf 1_m,\phi_1,\dots,\phi_n\}.
$$
Since the rank is already $n+1$, this containment is an equality.

Now
$$
B^T\mathbf 1_m = n\,\mathbf 1_N,
$$
because each cube vertex is incident to exactly $n$ poles. Therefore
$$
BB^T\mathbf 1_m = B(n\mathbf 1_N)=n\,2^{n-1}\mathbf 1_m.
$$
So $\mathbf 1_m$ is an eigenvector of $BB^T$ with eigenvalue $n2^{n-1}$.

Also, by Lemma 3,
$$
BB^T\phi_i = B\chi_{e_i} = 2^{n-1}\phi_i.
$$
Thus every axial mode $\phi_i$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $2^{n-1}$.

The vectors $\mathbf 1_m,\phi_1,\dots,\phi_n$ are linearly independent, their total
dimension is $n+1$, and this already equals $\mathrm{rank}(B)$. Hence these are
all nonzero eigenvalues of $BB^T$, with multiplicities exactly as stated.

`∎`

## 5. Theorem A. Complete Spectrum of the Block Graph

The spectrum of $L_{\mathrm{block}}$ splits into four sectors.

### 5.1. Coupled weight-0 sector

There are two weight-0 lines:

- the global constant mode,
- one nontrivial coupled line.

They give eigenvalues:
$$
0
\quad\text{and}\quad
n+2^{n-1}.
$$

---

### 5.2. Coupled weight-1 sector

For every axis $i$, the pair
$$
(\phi_i,\chi_{e_i})
$$
spans an invariant $2\times 2$ block:
$$
M_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
2(n-1)+2^{n-1} & -2^{n-1}\\
-1 & n+2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Its two eigenvalues are:
$$
\lambda_{w1}^{\pm}
=
\frac{T\pm\sqrt{\Delta}}{2},
$$
where
$$
T=3n+2^{n-1},
\qquad
\Delta=(n-4+2^{n-1})^2+2^{n+1}.
$$

Each has multiplicity $n$.

---

### 5.3. Decoupled weight-$k$ sectors

For every $k\ge 2$:
$$
\lambda=2k+n
$$
with multiplicity
$$
\binom{n}{k}.
$$

These are exactly the sectors $D_k$.

---

### 5.4. Pure octahedral sector

The orthogonal complement of the constant and axial octahedral modes gives:
$$
\lambda = 2n+2^{n-1}
$$
with multiplicity $n-1$.

---

### 5.5. Full spectrum table

| Sector | Eigenvalue | Multiplicity |
|:---|:---|:---:|
| Coupled weight-0 | $0$ | 1 |
| Coupled weight-0 | $n+2^{n-1}$ | 1 |
| Coupled weight-1 | $\lambda_{w1}^{-}$ | $n$ |
| Coupled weight-1 | $\lambda_{w1}^{+}$ | $n$ |
| Decoupled weight-$k$ | $2k+n$ | $\binom{n}{k}$ |
| Pure octahedral | $2n+2^{n-1}$ | $n-1$ |

The multiplicities sum to:
$$
1+1+n+n+\sum_{k=2}^n\binom{n}{k}+(n-1)=2n+2^n.
$$

So the decomposition is complete.

## 6. Boolean Functions and the Embedded Vector

Let
$$
f:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}
$$
be a Boolean function.

Its bipolar form is:
$$
f_{\pm}=2f-1\in\{-1,+1\}^{2^n}.
$$

We embed it into the block graph as:
$$
f_{\mathrm{block}}=(0_m,f_{\pm}).
$$

This is the universal fixed-graph viewpoint:

- the graph is fixed;
- the function does **not** define the graph;
- instead, the function is inserted into the cube-side of one common spectral object.

## 7. Theorem B. Correlation Immunity as Spectral Stratification

Recall the standard Walsh criterion:
$$
\mathrm{CI}(f)\ge t
\iff
W_f(u)=0
\quad\text{for all }u\text{ with }1\le \mathrm{wt}(u)\le t.
$$

In the block graph, this becomes:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{CI}(f)\ge t
\iff
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda_{w1}^{-}})=0
\ \wedge\
\forall k\in[2,t]:
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},D_k)=0
}
$$

For $n\ge 5$, where the relevant sectors are spectrally separated, this can be written as:
$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{CI}(f)\ge t
\iff
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda_{w1}^{-}})=0
\ \wedge\
\forall k\in[2,t]:
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda=2k+n})=0
}
$$

The simplification for $n\ge 5$ is justified by the explicit spectral ladder
$$
\lambda_{w1}^{-} < n+2 < n+4 \le 2k+n \le 3n < n+2^{n-1} < 2n+2^{n-1},
$$
valid for every $k\ge 2$.
Indeed:

- from
$$
  \sqrt{(n-4+2^{n-1})^2+2^{n+1}} > n-4+2^{n-1}
$$
  we get
$$
  \lambda_{w1}^{-} < n+2;
$$
- for $k\ge 2$,
$$
  2k+n \ge n+4;
$$
- and for $n\ge 5$,
$$
  3n < n+2^{n-1}
$$
  because $2^{n-1} > 2n$.

So from dimension $5$ onward the coupled weight-$1$ sector, the decoupled
weight-$k$ sectors, the coupled weight-$0$ line, and the pure octahedral
sector occupy pairwise distinct eigenvalues.

### Why this is true

- weight 1 is controlled by the coupled axial sector from Lemma 3;
- weights $2,\dots,t$ are controlled by the decoupled sectors from Lemma 2.

So the usual Walsh vanishing condition becomes vanishing on explicit spectral sectors of $L_{\mathrm{block}}$.

### 7.1. Why low-dimensional collisions are harmless

For $n=3$ and $n=4$, some eigenvalues collide:

- for $n=3$, the nontrivial weight-0 line $C_0^+$ and the decoupled sector $D_2$
  both lie at eigenvalue $\lambda=7$;
- for $n=4$, the same phenomenon occurs for $C_0^+$ and $D_4$, both lying at
  eigenvalue $\lambda=12$.

This does **not** invalidate Theorem B, because the theorem is stated using the
canonical decoupled sectors $D_k$, not the full eigenspaces $E_{\lambda=2k+n}$.
The two colliding pieces remain canonically separated.

Indeed:

- every vector in $D_k$ has the form $(0_m,\chi_u)$ and therefore has zero
  octahedral component;
- the vector spanning $C_0^+$ is
$$
  (2^{n-1}\mathbf 1_m,-n\mathbf 1_N),
$$
  whose octahedral component is nonzero.

Therefore
$$
C_0^+\cap D_k=\{0\}
$$
in the colliding cases, so the common eigenspace still decomposes as a direct sum
of two canonically distinguishable pieces:
$$
E_{\lambda}
=
C_0^+\oplus D_k.
$$

This is exactly why the all-$n$ formulation of Theorem B is written with
the decoupled sectors $D_k$, whereas the simplified eigenspace formulation is
reserved for $n\ge 5$.

For weight $1$ there is no analogous ambiguity: the coupled sector is generated by
the $n$ invariant $2\times 2$ blocks from Lemma 3, and its cube-side projections
span the full weight-1 Walsh space. Hence the CI criterion remains exact in
dimensions $n=3,4$, even though some higher-weight cube sectors may collide in
eigenvalue with the nontrivial weight-0 line.

### Meaning

Correlation immunity is no longer read only as a Walsh-table condition.
It becomes a **spectral non-resonance condition** with specific low-weight sectors of the block graph.

## 8. The Weight-0 Completion

The CI theorem controls Walsh weights
$$
1,\dots,t,
$$
but not weight
$$
0.
$$

Weight 0 is exactly what controls balancedness.

So to complete the low-weight package, we need the exact weight-0 sector.

### 8.1. Two coupled weight-0 lines

Define:
$$
C_0^-=\mathrm{Span}\{(\mathbf 1_m,\mathbf 1_N)\},
$$
$$
C_0^+=\mathrm{Span}\{(2^{n-1}\mathbf 1_m,-n\mathbf 1_N)\}.
$$

The first is the global constant mode.
The second is the nontrivial coupled weight-0 line.

## 9. Theorem C. Balancedness as a Spectral Condition

Balancedness is equivalent to:
$$
\sum_x f_{\pm}(x)=0.
$$

Since
$$
f_{\mathrm{block}}=(0_m,f_{\pm}),
$$
we have:
$$
\langle f_{\mathrm{block}},(\mathbf 1_m,\mathbf 1_N)\rangle
=
\sum_x f_{\pm}(x),
$$
and also
$$
\langle f_{\mathrm{block}},(2^{n-1}\mathbf 1_m,-n\mathbf 1_N)\rangle
=
-n\sum_x f_{\pm}(x).
$$

Therefore:
$$
\boxed{
f\text{ is balanced}
\iff
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},C_0^-)=0
\iff
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},C_0^+)=0
}
$$

For $n\ge 5$, the line $C_0^+$ is exactly the eigenspace
$$
E_{\lambda=n+2^{n-1}}.
$$

### Meaning

Balancedness is the missing weight-0 part of the same spectral picture.

## 10. Theorem D. Resilience as the Completed Low-Weight Package

By definition:
$$
\mathrm{Res}(f)\ge t
\iff
f\text{ is balanced and }\mathrm{CI}(f)\ge t.
$$

Combining Theorem B and Theorem C, we obtain:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{Res}(f)\ge t
\iff
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},C_0^+)=0
\ \wedge\
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda_{w1}^{-}})=0
\ \wedge\
\forall k\in[2,t]:
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},D_k)=0
}
$$

For $n\ge 5$, this becomes a pure eigenspace statement:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{Res}(f)\ge t
\iff
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda=n+2^{n-1}})=0
\ \wedge\
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda_{w1}^{-}})=0
\ \wedge\
\forall k\in[2,t]:
\mathrm{proj}(f_{\mathrm{block}},E_{\lambda=2k+n})=0
}
$$

### Meaning

Resilience is exactly the statement that the embedded Boolean function has no spectral mass in the entire dangerous low-weight package:

1. weight 0,
2. weight 1,
3. weights $2,\dots,t$.

So the current block graph does not merely detect CI.
It detects the whole low-weight Walsh package:

- balancedness,
- correlation immunity,
- resilience.
